Post-Higgsteria
===============


Despite the time that has passed since the official announcement from CERN on July 4, 2012, about the discovery of a new massive boson that would be a candidate for the elusive Higgs boson, the topic continues to be talked about.

.. youtube:: JAlgX4FNiyM
   :align: center

The official announcement, and even what we know about the data from said experiment, are not conclusive as to whether what was discovered corresponds properly to the Higgs boson; however, another series of data tends to favor the predictions of the standard model, and therefore to increase the expectation of a positive for this finding.

What was observed at the LHC indicated it was a boson given the characteristic decay into pairs of photons, and massive (the most massive of the known bosons) determined by the decay rates and energies detected, 125.3 GeV (+/-0.6 GeV) (something insignificant in everyday life, equivalent to :math:`5\cdot 10^{-16}` grams). At the time, the popular mention of 5 :math:`\sigma` (sigma) (exactly 4.9) generated great confusion, which for many was assumed as a statistical certainty of being the Higgs boson when it only corresponds to the certainty of the mass measurement of the found boson (whether or not it is Higgs).
The importance of the possibility that it corresponds to the Higgs stands out for constituting an additional element to ratify the standard model of particle physics and for being a fundamental piece in understanding the mass generation mechanisms (although it is not the only mechanism, it is the important one for fundamental particles). This video is quite useful for more detail and in popular language, to which I was invited.

.. youtube:: anlDNYFvELs
   :align: center

To this day we are still waiting for measurements of properties such as spin, parity and others, which together can give the uniqueness of the particles to confirm its discovery as a true fact. The ATLAS and CMS modules later announced the increase in the statistical confidence of the mass data of the found boson to 6 and 7 :math:`\sigma`.

Recent observations of decay rates of B-mesons into muons show lower values than expected in supersymmetric (SUSY) models but in agreement with the standard model. This leaves SUSY models with some exclusion, and the possibility of finding the smallest of 5 Higgs of the SUSY model out of expectation (apparently, for the moment there is no hope of new physics). However, some less popular SUSY models are at that limit.

But what comes after the Higgs? Well, high-energy physics still has a long way to go, in studies of matter-antimatter relationship (the problem of the asymmetry of their distribution and CPT symmetries), the study of dark matter, extra dimensions and reproduction of conditions for the study of the early universe in its first instants.
